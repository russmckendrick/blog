## *A Model Life* by Lonely Robot 🤖

### 🎛️ From sci-fi saga to something far more personal

By the time John Mitchell reached *A Model Life*, Lonely Robot had already built a reputation for sleek, space-tinged prog. This 2022 release takes a sharp turn inward. Mitchell wrote, recorded, produced, mixed and mastered the album himself at Outhouse Studios, with Craig Blundell adding drums, and he has said the whole thing came together in roughly four weeks. That speed matters. The record feels concentrated rather than rushed, like a diary written in one intense burst.

What changed is the subject matter. Earlier Lonely Robot albums often leaned on cosmic narratives, but here Mitchell drops much of the sci-fi framing and writes far more directly about identity, pressure, memory and the false polish of modern life. The pandemic hangs over it too. He described it as a first message to a post-pandemic world, and you can hear that in the album’s sense of distance, unease and emotional stock-taking.

### 🎸 Modern prog with hooks, heart and a digital-age chill

Musically, *A Model Life* sits in that fertile modern prog space where strong songwriting matters as much as technical flair. Mitchell brings together prog rock, melodic rock, prog-pop and a touch of alternative atmosphere. The songs are built with care, but they do not disappear into self-indulgent noodling. Even when tracks stretch past the five-minute mark, they stay memorable.

His guitar work is a big part of the album’s character. Mitchell has spoken about drawing inspiration from David Gilmour and Joe Bonamassa, and you can hear that in the broad vibrato, singing lead lines and solos that aim for feeling first. The production is clean and present, very much a 2020s prog sound rather than a retro recreation. It has space, punch and detail, with vocals right at the centre.

### 🌐 Social media masks, isolation and human frailty

The title *A Model Life* points to one of the record’s sharpest ideas: the fantasy version of life people present online. Mitchell has linked the album to the endless positive affirmation of social media, that polished, spotless version of existence that rarely matches real experience. Songs such as “Digital God Machine” and “Species in Transition” push that further, asking what happens when technology begins to shape not just communication, but identity itself.

Then there is “The Island of Misfit Toys”, one of the clearest post-lockdown songs on the album, full of alienation and the feeling of not fitting the approved model. “Duty of Care” turns even more intimate, touching on Mitchell’s experience of being raised by adoptive parents. For a prog album, its complexity lies less in odd time signatures than in emotional weight and conceptual focus.

### 📰 A high point for Lonely Robot

Critics in the prog press were very taken with it. Reviews called it Mitchell’s most honest Lonely Robot record, and some went further, naming it among his best work full stop. Commercially, it lived where modern prog often lives, not in the mainstream charts, but in the strong, loyal world around InsideOut Music and the international prog audience.

Its legacy is still forming, but *A Model Life* already feels like a defining statement. It captures where contemporary prog has gone: genre-fluid, emotionally open, technically polished and fully aware of the anxieties of digital life.

## Affinity by Haken 🤖

### Recording it like a band, not a solo blueprint 🎛️

Released in April 2016, *Affinity* arrived at an interesting point for Haken. Earlier records often began with Richard Henshall demos, then grew through arrangement, but this one came together far more collectively. The band spent about a year writing it, building songs from the ground up as a group, which helps explain why the album feels so unified even when it jumps between metallic weight, glossy synths and huge choruses. It was also the first full Haken album with bassist Conner Green, after his earlier appearance on the *Restoration* EP.

Recording took place at Spacelab Studios in Germany, with the band producing and Jens Bogren supervising the sessions and handling mixing and mastering duties. Bogren’s touch matters here. *Affinity* sounds clean, punchy and modern, yet it leaves room for the small details, stacked vocals, keyboard textures, rhythmic turns, little bursts of melody, all of which can vanish on lesser prog-metal records.

### 80s futurism meets modern prog metal 🌌

What makes *Affinity* so distinctive is its love affair with the 1980s. Haken had often drawn from 70s prog before this, but here they leaned into a different set of touchstones, especially *90125*-era Yes, *Toto IV*, King Crimson’s *Three of a Perfect Pair*, and even Vince DiCola’s soundtrack to *The Transformers: The Movie*. You can hear that in the bright synth leads, clean chorused guitars and digital sheen running through tracks like “1985” and “The Endless Knot”.

Yet this is still unmistakably Haken. “Initiate” and “The Architect” have the odd metres, sharp dynamic shifts and technical control that prog fans want, while the hooks are far stronger than on many knotty genre albums. The result is prog metal that is happy to borrow from electronic pop, new wave and arena-rock polish without losing its teeth.

### Big ideas for an anxious, connected decade 📡

Although *Affinity* is often described as Haken’s first non-narrative album, it still has a clear conceptual pull. Its songs circle around technology, artificial intelligence, creator-and-created relationships, and the uneasy question of whether all this connection actually leaves people more isolated. In 2016, that felt especially timely. Streaming had become routine, social media had turned everyday life into a constant feed, and public worry about algorithms, data and digital identity was growing fast.

Haken even leaned into that mood in the album’s presentation, redesigning their website to resemble an old 80s operating system. That retro-computer aesthetic gave the record a clever double effect, nostalgic on the surface, uneasy underneath.

### Reception, reach and why it still matters 🚀

*Affinity* was warmly received and is often treated as Haken’s international breakout album. Reviewers praised how smoothly it joined technical metal with accessible songwriting, even when some felt the second half was a touch less immediate. Over time, it has become one of the key prog-metal records of the 2010s.

Its legacy lies in how naturally it handles genre-blending. This album helped prove that modern prog could absorb synth-pop colour and streaming-era immediacy while keeping long-form ambition intact. And “The Architect”, a vast centrepiece full of rhythmic twists and philosophical weight, remains one of Haken’s boldest statements.

## (the best of) New Order by New Order 🎛️

### A compilation born from a pause ⏸️
Released in November 1994 in the UK, with a US edition following in March 1995, *(the best of) NewOrder* arrived at a strange moment for the band. New Order had come through the tense, expensive sessions for *Republic*, then drifted into a hiatus. Rather than wait for a fresh chapter, London Records pulled together a career survey that worked as both a hits package and a reminder of just how much ground the group had covered.

This is not a simple copy-and-paste job from *Substance*. The focus falls mainly on the singles era from 1985 onwards, and the set includes 1994 reworkings of “True Faith”, “Bizarre Love Triangle”, “1963” and “Round & Round”. That matters, because New Order had always treated songs as living things. Single edits, 12-inch versions and remixes were part of the art, not afterthoughts. Even “Vanishing Point”, not a single, made the cut after becoming familiar as the theme to the BBC drama *Making Out*.

### The sound, synths and cool ache 💿
What makes this compilation distinctive is how clearly it captures New Order’s balancing act. These songs are built from drum machines, sequencers, glassy synth tones and taut basslines, yet they never feel sterile. Bernard Sumner’s plain, slightly fragile voice gives them a human centre. Peter Hook’s bass often carries the melody in that high, urgent way only he could manage. Stephen Morris keeps the pulse mechanical but lively, and Gillian Gilbert’s keyboards provide much of the sheen.

Across the set you hear New Order’s favourite trick: taking the emotional distance of post-punk and feeding it through pop hooks and club rhythm. “Bizarre Love Triangle”, “True Faith”, “Blue Monday 88” and “Regret” all have that clean, crisp production, cool surfaces, and a faint sadness underneath. It is synth-pop with a nightclub body and an indie heart.

### Reception and the mid-90s context 📈
Commercially, the album did very well, reaching the UK Top 5. Critics were warm too, even if some felt *Substance* remained the more definitive overview. That is fair. *Substance* tells the earlier story with more sweep, while *(the best of) NewOrder* catches the polished, radio-ready phase.

Its timing is interesting. In the middle of the grunge and alternative rock boom, New Order sounded like proof that “alternative” did not mean one thing. While guitar bands dominated headlines, this compilation reminded listeners that electronic pop, dance music and indie attitude could share the same space.

### Indie spirit and long afterlife ✨
Even at their most chart-friendly, New Order never lost their Factory Records spirit. They came from a world where experimentation, club culture and self-definition mattered more than fitting neat market categories. That DIY streak runs through this compilation, especially in its respect for alternate mixes and its refusal to separate pop songs from dance tracks.

Its legacy is easy to hear in later indie-dance, synth-pop revival and electronic rock. So many bands borrowed this recipe: melancholy melody, machine rhythm, bright synth colour, emotional restraint. This album captures the point where New Order had already changed pop, and still sounded completely like themselves.

## YEARNALISM by Baby Rose 🎙️

    layout={{

### A fast, instinctive recording process ✨

Baby Rose made *YEARNALISM* quickly, and that speed matters to how the album feels. She said that nine of its 12 songs were finished in the last half-hour, or hour, of a session, which gives the record a real snap of instinct rather than something overworked. The project began taking shape in London with producer Miles C. James, then grew during a near two-week studio camp at 64 Sound in Los Angeles. That in-person setting seems central to the album’s warmth. This was not a pandemic-era patchwork built over endless file transfers. It came from musicians in rooms together, reacting in real time.

That spontaneity suits Baby Rose perfectly. Her songs often live in the space between restraint and emotional overflow, and *YEARNALISM* catches that tension beautifully. Co-executive producer Biako Bueno, along with collaborators such as Tom Brenneck and Ryan James Carr, helped build arrangements that feel lived-in rather than polished to a shine.

### Desire, soul, and genre drift 🌙

The title says a lot. *YEARNALISM* is an album about longing, not in a vague way, but as a daily condition. Baby Rose described it as a study of desire in all its forms, and you can hear that in songs that move through romance, absence, doubt, memory, and self-possession.

Musically, it slips across borders with ease. There is classic R&B and deep soul at its centre, but also jazz phrasing, gospel feeling, soft-rock airiness, and even a country tint in places. That fluidity makes the album feel very 2020s. Contemporary R&B often refuses neat category labels, and Baby Rose uses that freedom with real purpose. Each shift in style feels tied to the emotional state of a song, rather than a genre exercise.

### The voice at the centre 🔥

What makes Baby Rose instantly recognisable is that remarkable contralto. It is low, smoky, bruised, and full of grain. Reviewers reached for names like Nina Simone, Billie Holiday, Tracy Chapman and Etta James, which tells you a lot about the emotional weight she carries in a single phrase.

The production gives her room. Instead of crowding the songs with slick digital clutter, *YEARNALISM* leans towards live instrumentation and analogue warmth. That choice keeps the focus on breath, tone, and phrasing. Even when the arrangements widen, her voice remains the anchor, sounding intimate and almost conversational one moment, then close to collapse the next.

### Reception and why it matters now 🖤

Critics greeted *YEARNALISM* warmly. Pitchfork focused on its many shades of desire, NPR praised the way longing shapes the songs, and *The Guardian* picked up on the dramatic emotional force in the performances. Commercially, hard chart data is still thin, but artistically the album already feels important.

Its wider meaning is cultural as much as musical. In an era of flattened online feeling, *YEARNALISM* leans into vulnerability, humanity, and old-school musical touch. That gives the record a quiet force. It suggests that modern R&B still has plenty of room for raw voices, genre-crossing ideas, and songs that sit with yearning instead of rushing to resolve it.

## (the rest of) NewOrder by New Order 🎛️

<LightGallery
    layout={{
        imgs: [

### A remix album with a real point 📀
Released in August 1995, *(the rest of) NewOrder* arrived as the companion to 1994’s *(the best of) NewOrder*. That pairing tells you a lot about its purpose. The first collection gathered the songs, this one gathered the afterlife of those songs, the club mixes, extended versions and fresh rethinks. About half the material had already existed in some form, while the rest was commissioned for the album, which gave it more shape than a random odds-and-ends release.

The headline attraction was “Blue Monday-95”, a new Hardfloor remix of the band’s most famous single. That choice was smart and very 1995. Hardfloor were acid techno specialists, so New Order were not simply polishing old work, they were putting it into conversation with the current dance underground. Across the record, songs from different periods, from early material like “Everything’s Gone Green” to *Republic* singles such as “Regret” and “Spooky”, were reframed for a decade obsessed with remixes, club culture and genre-hopping.

### Post-punk bones, club pulse 💿
What makes the album distinctive is that, even under fresh layers of house and techno production, New Order still sound unmistakably like New Order. Bernard Sumner’s cool, faintly wistful vocals remain intact. Peter Hook’s melodic sense still haunts the low end, even when the mixes lean harder into sequenced bass and programmed kicks. The emotional tone is classic New Order, dance music with a lonely streak.

That mix of feeling and machine rhythm had always been their gift, but here it gets pushed further towards the dancefloor. “Confusion” fits naturally because it already had electro in its DNA, thanks to Arthur Baker and John Robie. “True Faith” and “Regret” gain fresh club muscle without losing their ache. Even the older tracks carry that art-school tension, crisp design, emotional distance, functional rhythm, everything carefully arranged yet never cold.

### Reception, charts and the 90s context 📈
This was not treated like a minor side project. The album reached No. 5 in the UK, which is impressive for a remix compilation, and “Blue Monday-95” also became a hit single. That success says a lot about the mid-90s. Britpop was rising, grunge had already changed what “alternative” meant, and dance music had become impossible to ignore. New Order fitted neatly between those worlds.

They were never a grunge band, of course, but this album worked as a reminder that alternative music in the 90s did not belong only to guitars. New Order had been mixing indie instincts with club sound long before that crossover became normal. In that sense, *(the rest of) NewOrder* feels like both a retrospective and a quiet argument.

### Legacy and the band’s independent streak 🖍️
The album also carries New Order’s old Factory-era spirit. Even on a London Records release, there is still a sense of experimentation and self-reinvention rather than routine catalogue management. They treated remixes as part of the artwork, not a sales trick. That comes straight from post-punk thinking, where presentation, sound and concept all mattered.

Its legacy is tied to that idea. *(the rest of) NewOrder* helped confirm that New Order were not just an 80s singles band. They were a bridge between post-punk, synth-pop, indie and club music, and this album lets you hear that bridge in motion.

## *Little Earthquakes* by Tori Amos 🎹

<LightGallery
    layout={{
        imgs: [

    }}
/>


### Recording a debut the label nearly missed 🔧

Released in early 1992, *Little Earthquakes* came out after a messy, stubborn birth. Tori Amos had already lived through one false start with the glossy late-80s band Y Kant Tori Read, and this solo debut grew from the wreckage of that disappointment. Atlantic did not immediately hear what she was doing. A piano-led record with sharp, confessional songs did not fit a market chasing guitars and grunge. The album was rejected and reshaped more than once before it finally reached the finish line.

That long process left its mark on the record. Some tracks came from early sessions with Davitt Sigerson, others were worked up with Eric Rosse, often in his home studio, and a final stretch took place in London with Ian Stanley of Tears for Fears. That patchwork history sounds awkward on paper, yet the album feels remarkably personal and direct. Amos kept the core intact. She fought for the songs, played small gigs in Britain, and built an audience the old-fashioned way while the record was still being pieced together.

### Piano as alternative rock weapon 🎼

What makes *Little Earthquakes* distinctive is how it takes the emotional force of alternative rock and puts it through piano, not riff-heavy guitars. Amos’s voice moves from a hush to a near snarl, and the arrangements know when to stay bare and when to press harder. “Silent All These Years” floats with wounded calm, “Precious Things” burns with fury, and “Winter” has the ache of a memory you cannot quite put down.

The writing is just as striking. Religion, shame, desire, family, self-doubt and sexual violence all appear without softening the edges. “Me and a Gun”, sung a cappella, remains one of the starkest songs on a major-label debut from that period. In rock terms, that was a bold act. Amos was not playing by the usual rules of cool, distance or swagger.

### Reception, slow-burn success and the early-90s moment 📈

The album was praised strongly, especially in the UK first, then in the US as word spread. It did not explode like *Nevermind*, but it lasted. *Little Earthquakes* reached the UK Top 20, climbed more modestly in America, and went on to sell in the millions, with double-platinum status in the US.

Its timing matters. In the grunge era, Amos offered a different kind of alternative. She shared that scene’s dislike of polish and pretence, but her answer was intimacy rather than noise. The DIY spirit sits in the way the album was assembled through persistence, home recording, club gigs and sheer refusal to become what the label expected.

### Why its influence still feels real 🌍

*Little Earthquakes* helped open space for a wave of 90s female songwriters who wrote frankly about inner life, power, sex and damage. You can hear its echo in artists who learned that a song could be delicate and confrontational at once.

Its legacy is simple. This album widened the idea of what rock could hold. In the middle of the alternative boom, Tori Amos proved that a piano, an uncompromising voice and a set of deeply private songs could hit with the force of any loud band.

## The Sophtware Slump by Grandaddy 🤖🌾

<LightGallery
    layout={{

### Recording a machine-age daydream 🏚️🎛️

Released in May 2000, *The Sophtware Slump* came from a very unglamorous place: Jason Lytle largely made it alone in a remote farmhouse near Modesto, teaching himself gear, stacking parts, and mixing much of it himself. That matters, because the album sounds like it was built by one tired, funny, obsessive person trying to make sense of a world filling up with screens and static. Grandaddy had started as a rougher indie-rock band, but here Lytle pushed towards a more electronic and atmospheric sound, inspired in part by the door that *OK Computer* had opened.

The title itself is a joke with a sting. It twists “sophomore slump” into “sophtware”, turning industry pressure into a wider feeling of system failure. You can hear that tension everywhere: homemade recording methods, big concept-album ambition, and songs that feel both fragile and oddly cosmic. For a record so concerned with technology, its creation was deeply human and gloriously imperfect.

### Rustic futurism and songs full of static 🌌🎹

What makes *The Sophtware Slump* so distinctive is the way it joins two worlds that should not fit together. It has dusty guitars, sweet melodies and a slightly worn, Californian sadness, but it also has wheezing synths, mechanical textures and titles that sound as if they were pulled from a dying desktop computer. The result is indie rock with a sci-fi hangover.

Tracks like “The Crystal Lake”, “Hewlett’s Daughter” and “He’s Simple, He’s Dumb, He’s the Pilot” balance humour, melancholy and genuine beauty. Lytle writes about technology without sounding cold or academic. His songs are full of failed communication, loneliness and the creeping sense that progress has made life stranger rather than better. One critic memorably compared the album’s mood to “January 2, 2000”, after the millennial hype had faded and ordinary disappointment set in.

### Critics loved it, sales were modest 📻📰

The album was greeted with strong reviews and quickly became Grandaddy’s signature release. In Britain it connected especially well, reaching the Top 40 of the albums chart in its first week, and singles such as “The Crystal Lake” gave the band a real foothold. Commercially, though, this was never a blockbuster. It sold steadily rather than massively, which suited the record’s outsider charm.

That modest success also says a lot about the changing music business around 2000. Grandaddy were on V2, operating between indie values and bigger-label expectations. *The Sophtware Slump* arrived just as digital culture was changing how music was discussed, shared and imagined, and its themes felt uncannily tuned to that shift.

### Why it still feels so current 💾❤️

Its legacy rests on more than critical affection. This album caught tech-age unease early, before smartphones and social media made that anxiety feel unavoidable. It even gained extra weight after 9/11, when its mood of fragility, disappointment and mistrust in modern systems felt newly apt.

Plenty of later indie records borrowed its mix of lo-fi warmth, synth haze and suburban dread, but few matched its emotional clarity. *The Sophtware Slump* is still a rare thing: a DIY concept album about digital alienation that never forgets the person at the keyboard.

## It’s Great When You’re Straight...Yeah by Black Grape 🎉

<LightGallery
    layout={{
        imgs: [
            { src: "/assets/2026-07-20-listened-to-this-week/albums/Its-Great-When-Youre-Straight-Yeah.jpg", alt: "It&#39;s Great When You&#39;re Straight...Yeah by Black Grape" },

### A comeback built from chaos 🛠️

Released in August 1995, *It’s Great When You’re Straight...Yeah* arrived after the wreckage of Happy Mondays, with Shaun Ryder and Bez starting again in Black Grape alongside Kermit, formerly of Ruthless Rap Assassins. That line-up matters, because Kermit helped push the music far beyond baggy nostalgia. The album was recorded over roughly seven weeks in late 1994 and early 1995, with sessions at Rockfield, Chapel Studios and Boundary Row, then mixed in Los Angeles. Danny Saber and Stephen Lironi were central to its shape, bringing programming, samples and a much sharper studio attack.

What makes the creation story so good is how unpolished the method sounds. The band worked in a loose, fast way, feeding off jams, vocal exchanges and Ryder’s scattershot lyric style. There was a proper indie spirit in that approach, even if the album came out through a major-label set-up. It feels made by a gang rather than a committee, scrappy in attitude but clever in construction.

### Funk, rap, acid house, swagger 💥

This is one of the strangest records ever to land in the middle of Britpop and hit so hard. Instead of copying the Kinks, the Beatles or glam rock, Black Grape threw together dance-rock, funk, hip-hop, dub, acid house and a rough-edged rock punch. One account of the sessions summed up the idea as something like Cypress Hill with a Rolling Stones streak, which is about right.

Ryder’s half-snarled delivery rubs against Kermit’s rap phrasing, and that contrast gives the album much of its character. The grooves matter as much as the guitars. The beats swing, the basslines strut, the samples keep things unstable, and the choruses still stick in your head. It is filthy, funny and oddly pop at the same time.

### A Britpop hit that refused Britpop rules 📈

The album went to No. 1 in the UK, went platinum, and spun off major singles including “Reverend Black Grape”, “In the Name of the Father” and “Kelly’s Heroes”. It was also shortlisted for the Mercury Prize, which tells you this was more than a tabloid curiosity built around Ryder’s notoriety.

Critics responded to its nerve. In a year when Britpop often leaned on selective nostalgia, Black Grape sounded current, mixed-up and unruly. It was tied to the wider alternative boom of the 1990s, but not to grunge. Where grunge often turned inward, this album lunged outward, toward club culture, street slang, sampler mischief and bad behaviour.

### Why it still feels unruly and fresh 🔥

Its legacy comes from that refusal to behave like a proper rock album. It challenged the idea that British guitar music had to be tidy, earnest or retro-minded. You can hear Madchester’s afterglow in it, but also a response to the decade’s musical free-for-all, where rap, rave, rock and dub kept spilling into one another.

Even the sleeve, a pop-art image of Carlos the Jackal, tells you Black Grape were after provocation, satire and a nasty laugh. That spirit runs through the whole record. It remains one of the great 90s comeback albums, and one of the few Britpop-era chart-toppers that still sounds like it might fall apart at any moment.
